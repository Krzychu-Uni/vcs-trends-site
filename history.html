Timeline (highlights)

1972 — SCCS

Early file-level version control, introduced change tracking concepts used later in many tools.

1982 — RCS

Delta storage and per-file revision tracking (local VCS model).

1986 — CVS

Added networked repositories and project-level collaboration (client-server model).

2000 — Subversion (SVN)

Introduced atomic commits, better renames and a more robust centralized model compared to CVS.

2005 — Git & Mercurial

Distributed VCS (DVCS) appeared — every developer keeps a full history and commits locally. Git became dominant. See the Linux/Git story in the report for background.

Why DVCS changed the game

  • Local commits: fast commits and local history even without network.
  • Cheap branching & merging: encouraging feature branches and parallel work.
  • Resilience: every clone is a backup — fewer single points of failure.

These points are discussed in the report under the "Centralized vs. Distributed" section.
